## How are the costs distributed? 
<section style="padding:40px; background:#eef3fb; border-radius:12px; margin-top:20px;">



<ul style="font-size:20px; line-height:1.6;">
  <li><strong>18.8%</strong> (= 402,800) of German companies provide training. The majority are small and medium-sized enterprises.</li>
  <li>The economy provides <strong>€9.7&nbsp;bn</strong> for vocational education and training (total net costs).</li>
  <li>Businesses train more than <strong>500,000</strong> new trainees each year, <strong>77%</strong> of which they take on.</li>
  <li>They invest around <strong>€26,210</strong> per trainee per year (45% of which is training remuneration).</li>
  <li><strong>69%</strong> of the funding invested is recouped through the productive contributions of trainees during the training.</li>
</ul>

<h3 style="font-size:24px; margin-top:20px;">Public expenditure on dual vocational education and training in 2023</h3>

<ul style="font-size:20px; line-height:1.6;">
  <li>Approx. <strong>€4.403&nbsp;bn</strong> in total</li>
  <li>Of which <strong>€3.497&nbsp;bn</strong> on around 1,550 vocational schools</li>
  <li>And <strong>€0.906&nbsp;bn</strong> on management, monitoring and support measures</li>
</ul>

<h3 style="font-size:24px; margin-top:20px;">Trainees</h3>

<ul style="font-size:20px; line-height:1.6;">
  <li>Receive an average training remuneration of approx. <strong>€1,133 (gross)</strong> per month (2025).</li>
  <li>Attend vocational school free of charge.</li>
</ul>

<p style="font-size:16px; margin-top:20px;">
<em>Sources: BIBB Data Report to accompany the Report on Vocational Education and Training (2025 and 2024), BIBB Report 2/2025.</em>
</p>

## Gross costs incurred by trainees 
<section style="padding:40px; background:#f5f5f5; border-radius:12px; margin-top:20px;">

<h2 style="font-size:30px;">2.a) What gross costs are incurred by trainees?</h2>

<h3 style="font-size:24px; margin-top:10px;">Gross costs</h3>

<ul style="font-size:20px; line-height:1.6;">
  <li><strong>Personnel costs of trainees (~ 60%)</strong>
    <ul style="margin-top:5px;">
      <li>Training allowance (45%)</li>
      <li>Social contributions based on collective agreements</li>
      <li>Voluntary social contributions</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li><strong>Personnel costs of trainers (~ 25%)</strong>
    <ul style="margin-top:5px;">
      <li>Full-time trainer</li>
      <li>Part-time trainer</li>
      <li>External trainers</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li><strong>Capital expenditure and material costs (~ 4%)</strong>
    <ul style="margin-top:5px;">
      <li>Workplace (tools, equipment, practice material)</li>
      <li>Workshop</li>
      <li>In-house teaching</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li><strong>Other costs (~ 11%)</strong>
    <ul style="margin-top:5px;">
      <li>Teaching and learning materials/media</li>
      <li>Work and protective clothing</li>
      <li>Training administration</li>
      <li>External training</li>
      <li>Chamber/examination fees</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<p style="font-size:16px; margin-top:20px;">
<em>Sources: BIBB Data Report to accompany the Report on Vocational Education and Training (2025 and 2024), BIBB Report 2/2025.</em>

## Question 1: Cost Distribution in the Dual System

According to the GOVET data, how are the training costs distributed between companies and the public sector in Germany's dual VET system, and what does each party primarily finance?

[[ ]] Companies pay 50% (vocational schools), public sector pays 50% (workplace training)
[[X]] Companies pay approximately 70% (workplace training, trainee allowances), public sector pays approximately 30% (vocational schools)
[[ ]] Companies pay 30% (trainee supervision), public sector pays 70% (all instruction and materials)
[[ ]] Cost distribution is equal at 50/50 between companies and public sector
***
<section>

**Correct!** The dual system reflects a genuine partnership with distinct financial responsibilities:

**Company Investment (~70%):**
- Training allowance to trainees (compensation)
- Training personnel costs (full-time and part-time trainers)
- Training infrastructure (workshops, tools, equipment)
- Training materials and administrative costs
- Chamber and examination fees
- **Total company investment:** €9.7 billion net costs annually

**Public Sector Investment (~30%):**
- Vocational school infrastructure (buildings, maintenance)
- Teaching personnel (vocational school teachers)
- Teaching materials for schools
- Management, monitoring, and support measures
- **Total public expenditure (2023):** approximately €4.403 billion
  - €3.497 billion on 1,550 vocational schools
  - €0.906 billion on management and support measures

**Key Insight:**
This cost-sharing model is fundamental to the sustainability of the dual system. Companies make the larger investment because they directly benefit from trained workers, while the state ensures universal access through free vocational schooling.

**Important Statistics:**
- 18.8% (402,800) of German companies provide training
- Majority are small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)
- Companies invest approximately €26,210 per trainee per year
- 45% of company costs are training remuneration
- **69% of funding is recouped through productive trainee contributions during training**

**Source:** GOVET Costs & Benefits Presentation 2025, BIBB Data Report 2025

</section>

## Question 2: Gross Costs Structure

What percentage of gross training costs is typically attributed to personnel costs of trainees (including training allowance and social contributions)?

[[ ]] Approximately 35-40%
[[ ]] Approximately 45-50%
[[X]] Approximately 60-61%
[[ ]] Approximately 75-80%
***
<section>

**Correct!** The cost structure of dual VET training reveals important priorities:

**Breakdown of Gross Costs:**

**1. Personnel Costs of Trainees (~60-61%):**
- Training allowance (45% of total gross costs)
- Social contributions based on collective agreements
- Voluntary social contributions
- Average training remuneration: ~€1,133 gross per month (2025)

**2. Personnel Costs of Trainers (~24-25%):**
- Full-time trainers
- Part-time trainers
- External trainers

**3. Capital Expenditure and Material Costs (~4%):**
- Workplace equipment (tools, practice materials)
- Training workshops
- In-house teaching facilities

**4. Other Costs (~11%):**
- Teaching and learning materials/media
- Work and protective clothing
- Training administration
- External training courses
- Chamber and examination fees

**Economic Significance:**
The high percentage of personnel costs (trainees + trainers = ~85%) demonstrates that dual VET is fundamentally a **labor-intensive investment** rather than a capital-intensive one. This structure:
- Emphasizes the human development aspect
- Reflects fair compensation for trainees' work
- Shows significant trainer involvement in the process
- Makes the system adaptable to different sectors and company sizes

**Critical Analysis Point:**
Despite the 45% spent on training allowance, trainees recoup significant value through their productive contributions (69% return rate), making this a mutual investment rather than pure cost.

**Source:** GOVET Costs & Benefits Presentation 2025, BIBB Report 2/2025

</section>

## Question 3: Return on Investment During Training

During the training period, trainees perform various types of work that generate returns for companies. How is this productive work typically distributed between unskilled and skilled activities?

[[ ]] 20% unskilled activities, 80% skilled worker activities
[[ ]] 50% unskilled activities, 50% skilled worker activities
[[X]] 39% unskilled activities, 59% skilled worker activities
[[ ]] 70% unskilled activities, 30% skilled worker activities
***
<section>

**Correct!** The productive contribution of trainees is a key economic factor in the dual VET model:

**Distribution of Productive Work:**

**Unskilled Activities (39%):**
- Generates savings equivalent to hiring an unskilled worker
- More common in early training phases
- Includes basic operational tasks and support functions

**Skilled Worker Activities (59%):**
- Generates savings equivalent to hiring a skilled worker
- Increases as training progresses
- Represents real value creation for the company

**Additional Returns (~2%):**
- ~1% productive contributions in training workshops
- ~1% subsidies (government/Federal Employment Agency/ESF*)

**Economic Impact:**
This distribution is crucial because:
1. **69% of invested funding is recouped** through these productive contributions during training
2. The high percentage of skilled activities (59%) shows trainees quickly become valuable contributors
3. Companies receive immediate short-term returns while investing in long-term workforce development

**Progressive Development Model:**
The presentation shows that **net costs decline significantly over the three-year training period** as trainee productivity increases:
- **Year 1:** Higher net costs (trainees still learning)
- **Year 2:** Reduced net costs (increasing competence)
- **Year 3:** Lowest net costs (near-skilled worker productivity)
- **Total average net costs over 3 years:** €22,927 per trainee

**Strategic Implication:**
This return structure makes dual VET economically viable for companies, especially when combined with long-term benefits (retention, company-specific skills, selection advantages).

## Question 5: Comparative Economic Analysis - Training vs. Recruiting

According to the economic logic presented in the GOVET framework, why is training apprentices often more cost-effective for companies than recruiting already-qualified skilled workers from the external labor market?

[[ ]] Training is always cheaper because trainee allowances are lower than skilled worker salaries
[[X]] Training provides combined benefits: productivity during training, savings on recruitment/induction costs, retention advantages, and company-specific skill development
[[ ]] Government subsidies cover most training costs, making it essentially free for companies
[[ ]] External skilled workers are unavailable in Germany due to the dual VET system's success
***
<section>

**Correct!** The economic case for training versus recruiting reflects a sophisticated cost-benefit analysis:

**Training Model - Comprehensive Benefits:**

**Short-Term Returns During Training:**
- 69% of costs recouped through productive work
- 59% skilled worker activities + 39% unskilled activities
- Progressive productivity increase across training years
- Immediate operational contributions

**Long-Term Savings on Recruitment:**
Companies avoid multiple cost categories:
- **Advertising costs** (job postings, agency fees)
- **Screening costs** (CV review, background checks)
- **Interview costs** (time, personnel involvement)
- **Final selection costs** (assessment centers, testing)

**Induction and Integration Costs Avoided:**
- **Onboarding costs** (orientation programs, documentation)
- **Lower productivity** of existing skilled workers involved in induction
- **Continuing training costs** to bridge skill gaps
- **Productivity differences** during adjustment period
- **Cultural integration** challenges with external hires

**Strategic Advantages Unique to Training:**

**1. Company-Specific Skills:**
- Trainees learn systems, processes, and culture from day one
- No "unlearning" of previous employer's methods
- Perfect alignment with company standards

**2. Selection Quality:**
- 3-year evaluation period versus brief hiring process
- Real performance assessment under actual conditions
- Mutual compatibility testing

**3. Loyalty and Retention:**
- 77% retention rate of trained workers
- Emotional investment reduces turnover
- Lower lifetime recruitment costs

**4. Skilled Worker Shortage Prevention:**
- Independence from tight labor markets
- Workforce planning predictability
- Future leadership pipeline

**Economic Reality Check:**
- **Average net cost per trainee over 3 years:** €22,927
- **Comparable recruitment and induction costs** for skilled workers often equal or exceed this
- **Retention benefit:** Trained workers stay longer, reducing repeated recruitment costs
- **Quality benefit:** Custom-trained workers are more productive faster than external hires

**Market Context:**
The presentation emphasizes that skilled worker shortages make external recruitment increasingly expensive and uncertain. Training provides **greater flexibility with vacancies** and reduces dependency on volatile labor markets.

**Formula for Economic Viability:**
```
Training Investment = 
  Gross Costs 
  - Productive Returns (69%)
  - Recruitment Savings
  - Induction Savings
  + Retention Benefits
  + Company-Specific Skill Value
  + Selection Quality Improvement
  = NET POSITIVE VALUE
```

**Broader Economic Impact:**
The presentation concludes with social and economic benefits including:
- Large pool of qualified skilled workers
- High flexibility and mobility due to standardized qualifications
- Strong productivity growth
- Increased economic competitiveness
- Social stability through low youth unemployment

**Quote from Presentation:**
*"There is only one thing in the long run more expensive than education: no education."* — John F. Kennedy
